What is Wishbone?

A Fantasy Football Tool

Wishbone is an extension of fantasy football's trading system.

It's essentially a roster management application built around the "trading block." Wishbone is built to automate the trading process between league members and facilitate their commissioner's administration duties.

A Trading Engine

Wishbone is a trade-finding, player-swapping, server-side program that runs automatically. It lives in the cloud and runs in perpetuity, notifying users of potential trades as they arise.

Wishbone can find trades for its users without them logged in.

Why would anyone need this?

Because fantasy trading hasn't been automated.

When fantasy football made the leap from pen & paper (remember those?) to PC, almost all the difficult parts of participating in a league were made easy.

Back in the dark ages, mangers had to manually tabulate scores, draft on hunches, mail in roster changes, and communicate trade proposals via tin cups and string.

Scoring? Not only is it automated, but it's live. Now, you can watch your team lose on your iPad, in real time!

Drafting? Just set your preferences, switch on auto-pilot, and let the computer do all the work. (If you're insane, that is.)

Managing? Believe it or not, if you pay some fantasy football services, they'll let you use their mathematical prediction models and give you the most statistically likely lineup to win your game based on your opponent. They'll do all the work for you short of physically clicking your players into their slots.

But the Trading Block? Across all fantasy sports, the trading block barely evolved beyond the carrier pigeons of yore. Today, managers still have to propose trades via email - one email per proposal.

Since fantasy football has been otherwise automated, trading has become a slow, arduous process with very little return on the time investment it takes to set a trade up.

The Ideal Scenario

Step 1: Proposal

A team manager proposes a trade to the manager of another team.

Step 2: Reply

The manager of team B promptly responds and accepts the proposed trade.

Step 3: Trade Complete

The managers swap their players, and everyone is happy.

What Actually Happens

Step 1: Proposal

A team manager proposes a trade to the manager of another team.

Step 2: Awkward Silence

Almost an entire week goes by before the manager of team B speaks up, if they do at all.

Step 3: Badgering

The manager of team A, desperate for a response, blasts the manager of team B with a flurry of emails, texts, and phone calls.

Step 4: Trade Declined

Probably because their silence was meant to be interpreted as disinterest, team B replies in the negative.

Step 5: #%$*#!

The manager of team A is forced to keep searching for a trade or drop the player on the block. The deadline to complete transactions is fast approaching, and no one is happy.

Return to step 1.

...and have you ever tried this?

Fun for exactly zero people.

That's where Wishbone comes in.

Wishbone does the heavy lifting

Wishbone collects each team's roster of players.

The league managers mark their players as either "On The Block" (tradable) or "Keepers" (untouchable).

Managers then mark players they want on other teams as "Targeted," and players they specifically don't want as "Unwanted."

Wishbone churns through those designations and notifies managers if there are trades on the table.

If all parties in a trade agree to the terms, it happens! It's that simple.

Planning

Brand Development

Everything from the logo to the typefaces to the color scheme was planned before design took place. Wishbone has a full-on brand style guide, replete with usage examples, grayscales, and two-color fallbacks.

Wireframing

Wireframes were built and refined using Adobe Illustrator. Once designed, the wireframes were immediately rendered in HTML and CSS.

Prototyping

Wishbone's concept was prototyped using card sorting and a bunch of twine. (No joke.) After that, prototypes were rapidly built in live HTML, on top of the wireframes.

Design

Clean, Flat, Simple

Wishbone's design was inspired by Google's Holo Theme, as well as some elements pulled from Twitter. Overall, Wishbone seeks to be clean, flat, and simple; to present an interface that gets out of the way and lets users do their thing.

Mobile in Mind

Although Wishbone is not a native application, the interface is designed to function within mobile browsers.

All image assets exist as Scalable Vector Graphics (SVGs) that render in perfect resolution no matter the screen.

Development

Tools of the Trade

Wishbone was written entirely within the wonderful SublimeText 2 text editor. No WYSIWYGs here.

Stylesheets were written, precompiled, and minified using SASS.

The project exists as a repo on GitHub, essential to version control.

Built on the Shoulders of Giants

Wishbone relies on brilliant frameworks and libraries to do its job:

  • Underscore.js for templates and sorting data
  • Parse (a fork of Backbone.js) for managing that data in the cloud
  • jQuery for manipulating that data in the DOM
  • Inuit.scss to make the app accessible across as many devices as possible.
